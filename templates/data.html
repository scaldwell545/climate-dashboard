"For most of the past 800,000 years—much longer than human civilization has existed—the concentration of greenhouse gases in our atmosphere was between about 200 and 280 parts per million. (In other words, there were 200 to 280 molecules of the gases per million molecules of air).

"But in the past century, that concentration has jumped to more than 400 parts per million, driven up by human activities such as burning fossil fuels and deforestation. The higher concentrations of greenhouse gases—and carbon dioxide in particular—is causing extra heat to be trapped and global temperatures to rise."

—Natural Resources Defense Council, Inc.

Greenhouse Gas Emissions by Sector and Country


Per the United States Energy Information Administration, greenhouse gases absorb infrared radiation and trap its heat in the atmosphere, creating a greenhouse effect that results in global warming and climate change. Some gases occur naturally and are also produced by human activities. Some, such as industrial gases, are exclusively human made.

Use the pie graph to examine the sector-by-sector breakdown of global greenhouse gas emissions in 2016. The bar graph illustrates how each of the top 20 countries/entities contribute to total global GHG emissions between 1961 and 2017.

"Changes in climate can result in impacts to air quality.
Atmospheric warming associated with climate change has the potential to increase ground-level ozone in many regions, which may present challenges for compliance with the ozone standards in the future.
However, the direct relationship between climate change on other air pollutants is unknown."

"Conversely, emissions of pollutants into the air can result in changes to the climate.
Ozone in the atmosphere warms the climate, while different components of particulate matter (PM) can have either warming or cooling effects on the climate."

—Environmental Protection Agency

Mean annual exposure of PM2.5 (fine particulate) air pollution is measured in micrograms per cubic meter.

Countries such as Nepal, Nigeria, Chad, China, and India are among the top 20 countries in mean PM2.5 air pollution. Mean PM2.5 air pollution for Europe, the world, and the U.S. are included for reference.

1 Ambient PM2.5 pollution is defined as the average level of exposure of a nation's population to concentrations of suspended particles measuring less than 2.5 microns in aerodynamic diameter.
